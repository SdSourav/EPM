Chapter 3 · Traditional PM

Traditional: hierarchy, plan, gates

  • Waterfall + Stage-Gate — gates = go / kill
  • Roles in a hierarchy: sponsor → PM → team
  • Strong control & governance
  • Weakness: rigid — hard to change
Cooper 2016 · Havermans 2019 · Vrhovec 20153 / 8
First, the traditional way. It is built on planning and control. The two main models are Waterfall and Stage-Gate. In Stage-Gate, at each gate a manager decides to continue or stop the project. The roles form a clear hierarchy. The sponsor is at the top — he gives the money and decides at the gates. The project manager is in the middle: he plans the work and handles all communication. The team does the work, and stakeholders shape the requirements. The strength is control and clear responsibility. The weakness is that it is rigid — hard to change.
Chapter 4 · Agile PM

Agile: flat, iterative, trust-based

  • Agile Manifesto 2001 — respond to change
  • Scrum: sprints · transparency · inspection · adaptation
  • PO = value · SM = facilitates (no authority)
  • Developers = self-organize, share work
Beck 2001 · Azanha 2017 · Kristensen & Paasivaara 2021 · Spiegler 20194 / 8
Now the agile way. It started in software, where requirements changed very fast. In 2001, the Agile Manifesto set out four values — for example, working software over documentation, and responding to change over following a plan. The most popular framework is Scrum. Scrum works in short cycles called sprints, and stands on three pillars: transparency, inspection, and adaptation. There is no hierarchy. Instead there are three roles: the Product Owner owns the value and the backlog; the Scrum Master helps the team but has no authority — he does not give orders; and the Developers organise their own work and share responsibility.
Chapter 5 · The comparison

Same goal, opposite mechanics

Structure: hierarchy ↔ flat  ·  Leadership: authority ↔ facilitation  ·  Communication: formal ↔ face-to-face

Context decides — hybrids (Agile–Stage-Gate) combine both: governance outside, agility inside.
McHugh 2012 · Moe 2010 · Shrivastava & Rathod 2015 · Cooper 20165 / 8
This compares the two. In structure, traditional is a hierarchy, agile is flat. In leadership, traditional gives authority to the manager and the gates, while agile leadership is facilitation that spreads to the team as it matures. Communication is formal in traditional, but frequent and face to face in agile — and that builds trust. The biggest difference is here: traditional fixes the scope and lets time and budget move; agile fixes time and budget, and lets the scope change. So which is better? It depends on the context. Stable projects fit traditional, uncertain projects fit agile, and many companies combine both in hybrids.
Chapter 6 · AI in project management

AI lands on the roles

In traditional PM

  • Planning & scheduling
  • Risk prediction (delays, overruns)
  • Resource allocation, reports

In agile PM

  • Scrum Master: summaries, impediments
  • Product Owner: backlog, user stories
  • Sprint planning from past data

= Augmented, not replaced — the manager becomes a decision facilitator

Niederman 2021 · Dam 2019 · Barcaui & Monat 20236 / 8
This is my chapter — AI in project management. AI lands on the roles in both worlds. In traditional management, AI helps with the structured parts: it automates planning and scheduling, predicts risks like delays, and helps allocate resources. In agile, AI helps the Scrum Master with daily summaries and spotting repeated problems, and helps the Product Owner prioritise the backlog and write user stories. But the key message is this: AI does not replace people — it augments them. The manager becomes a decision facilitator. AI does the data work; the human keeps the judgement, the ethics, and the leadership. One warning: if AI is used to watch individual people, it can destroy the trust that agile needs.
Chapter 6 · Challenges vs opportunities

What could go wrong — and right

Risks

  • Ethics — fairness, surveillance, accountability stays human
  • Data dependency — bad data → bad recommendations
  • Trust & adoption — rejection AND blind trust are both dangerous

Opportunities

  • Efficiency — admin work automated away
  • Better decisions — more data, patterns, foresight
  • Hybrid human–AI collaboration — digital intelligence as the new PM skill (Marnewick)
Marnewick 20217 / 8
With AI come risks and opportunities. The risks: ethics, like fairness and surveillance; data dependency — bad data gives bad results; and trust, because people may reject AI or trust it blindly. The opportunities: more efficiency, better decisions from more data, and a future where humans and AI work together. The important point: the human stays responsible — AI only gives recommendations.
Chapter 7 · Conclusion

Answering the research question

  • Roles differ by design: concentrated authority + formal communication vs distributed responsibility + trust built in ceremonies
  • Context decides the method — stable scope → traditional · uncertainty → agile · both → hybrids
  • AI augments every role — it automates the administrative layer and turns managers into decision facilitators, while judgment, ethics and leadership stay human
AI changes what the roles do — not why they exist.  Thank you — questions welcome.
