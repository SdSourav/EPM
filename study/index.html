What is Project Management? 📜 definition verbatim

Project management is the process of managing projects with the goal of completing them successfully — e.g. on time, on budget, within the agreed level of quality — using a variety of skills, tools and techniques to plan for and manage all activities needed to complete the project.

The iron triangle (trade-offs)

  • Managing projects involves trade-offs between Time, Scope and Cost (scope = the deliverable at the end of the project).
  • Changes in any one side of the triangle affect one or both other sides.
  • Scope creep = increasing the scope after a project has begun.
  • The project stakeholders decide the overriding "key success factor" (time, scope or cost); the PM is responsible for demonstrating the impact of the key success factors to stakeholders.
  • Progress is measured and tracked via time, cost, scope, resources, quality and risks.
The iron triangle (Lecture 1) — stakeholders pick the overriding key success factor; the PM demonstrates the trade-offs. Scope creep stretches the top corner after the project has begun.
🧠 Memory hook The street version of the iron triangle: "Good, fast, cheap — pick two." Good = scope/quality, fast = time, cheap = cost. And scope creep = the top corner quietly growing after kickoff.
⭐ Connect it forward This triangle is exactly what flips between Traditional and Agile PM: traditional fixes scope and flexes time/budget; Agile fixes time & budget (timeboxing) and flexes scope. See the Frameworks and Scrum tabs.

Break deliverables down into Work Packages (WPs) and estimate each with respect to cost, schedule, resources needed, and risks. WPs have dependencies (WP2 needs WP1…), and each WP ends in a milestone with a deliverable (e.g. Requirements→report, Design→graphical depiction, Code→source files, Test→analytics file).

⭐ Three-point estimating (be able to compute this!) 📜 formulas verbatim

E = (a + 4m + b) / 6 SD = (b − a) / 6 a = best case m = most likely b = worst case Slide example: a=30, m=40, b=90 → E = (30+160+90)/6 = 46.7 SD = (90−30)/6 = 10 Confidence: E ± 1·SD ≈ 68% · E ± 2·SD ≈ 95% · E ± 3·SD ≈ 99.7%
The slide's worked example as a distribution: E = (30 + 4·40 + 90)/6 = 46.7, SD = (90−30)/6 = 10. The skew is the point — the worst case (90) sits far right, which is why you never estimate with the "confident low-risk case".

Why not just use the confident low-risk case? Because estimates must carry their uncertainty; the weighted mean + SD makes the risk explicit.

🧠 Memory hooks The formula is "1-4-1 over 6" — best case once, most likely four times, worst case once, divide by six (1+4+1 = 6, so the division makes sense by itself). SD = "worst minus best over six." Confidence = the 68–95–99.7 staircase: 1σ "probably", 2σ "almost surely", 3σ "basically certain".

Critical path & PERT 📜 verbatim

  • Critical path — the longest necessary path of activities, which is also the shortest possible duration of the project. Any delay on it delays the project.
  • PERT (Program/Project Evaluation and Review Technique) — a statistical tool to analyze and represent the activities involved in completing a project (works with the three-point estimates above).
  • Visualize schedules with network diagrams and Gantt charts.
A minimal network diagram: C→D (4 days) has slack; any delay on A→B delays the whole project. PERT runs the three-point statistics over exactly this kind of network.

⭐ Agile–Stage-Gate hybrids (Cooper 2016; Cooper & Sommer 2016)

Leading manufacturers embed Agile (Scrum) inside the stages of their gating process: the gates and stages keep governance, while sprints, scrums and demos run inside each stage to build/test iteratively. Began in IT (where Agile and Stage-Gate were found to be complementary), now used for physical products (e.g. LEGO).

The Agile–Stage-Gate hybrid (Cooper 2016; Cooper & Sommer 2016) — governance outside, agility inside.
  • Benefits: much faster product releases, better response to changing customer requirements, improved team communication and morale.
  • The "Power of Nine" to make it work: 3 key artefacts (e.g. sprints & scrum ceremonies), 3 tools (e.g. sprint backlog, burndown chart), 3 roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, team).
  • Modifications needed for physical products: e.g. redefining a "done sprint" — the increment may be a model, design or prototype rather than working software shippable to a customer.

What Scrum is (and isn't)

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems. It is not a methodology, not a cookbook of best practices, and not a defined/predictive process — it is purposefully incomplete: it defines only the parts required to implement Scrum theory, and you fill in your own practices within it.

⭐ From your class quiz "Which statement best describes Scrum?" → A framework for creating complex products in complex environments. (Not a methodology, not a cookbook, not Scientific Management.)

Empiricism — the engine of Scrum

Scrum is founded on empiricism (knowledge comes from experience; decide based on what is observed) and lean thinking (reduce waste, focus on essentials). In complex work, more is unknown than known — so you discover the unknown by releasing Done increments early and often, validating assumptions, and adapting. That's how Scrum delivers more value sooner with less risk (the comic strip from your slides).

The Scrum Team — 3 accountabilities, 0 hierarchy

One Product Owner, one Scrum Master, and Developers — typically 10 or fewer people, cross-functional (all skills needed to create value) and self-managing (they internally decide who does what, when, and how). No sub-teams, no hierarchies. The whole team is accountable for creating a valuable, useful Increment every Sprint.

🧭 Product Owner

  • Accountable for maximizing the value of the product and for effective Product Backlog management: develops and explicitly communicates the Product Goal; creates, clearly communicates and orders Product Backlog items; ensures the backlog is transparent, visible and understood.
  • Is one person, not a committee; may delegate the work but remains accountable.
  • Those wanting to change the Product Backlog can do so by trying to convince the PO. Only the PO has authority to cancel a Sprint (if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete).

🎯 Scrum Master

  • Accountable for establishing Scrum as defined in the Scrum Guide and for the Scrum Team's effectiveness.
  • A servant leader (the guide says "true leaders who serve"): coaches self-management, helps focus on high-value increments, removes impediments, ensures events happen and stay within timeboxes.
  • Serves the PO (backlog techniques, empirical planning), the Developers, and the organization (training, coaching, removing barriers between stakeholders and teams).
  • Is not a manager, secretary, or status-reporter. Does not assign work.

💻 Developers

  • Anyone doing the work of creating the Increment (regardless of specialty — design, test, code…).
  • Accountable for: creating the Sprint Backlog (the plan for the Sprint); instilling quality by adhering to the Definition of Done; adapting their plan each day toward the Sprint Goal; holding each other accountable as professionals.
  • No one else tells them how to turn backlog items into Increments — work is pulled, never assigned.
⭐ From your class quiz
  • "Who manages the progress of work during a Sprint?" → The Developers.
  • "When does a Developer become sole owner of a Sprint Backlog item?" → Never — items are owned collectively by the Developers.
  • "When should a Developer be replaced?" → As needed, accepting a short-term productivity dip (teams are stable but not frozen).
  • "CEO demands a 'very important' item mid-Sprint?" → Inform the rest of the Scrum Team and decide together (scope is negotiated with the PO; nobody bypasses the team).

More Sprint rules that exams love

  • During the Sprint: no changes that endanger the Sprint Goal; quality does not decrease; the Product Backlog is refined as needed; scope may be clarified and renegotiated with the PO as more is learned.
  • Shorter Sprints = more learning cycles, less risk. A Sprint is never extended.
  • Only the Product Owner can cancel a Sprint (Sprint Goal obsolete).
  • Refinement is an ongoing activity, not an event — breaking down and further defining items (adding size, order, description).

Scrum in depth: multiple teams, one product

When products grow, you add more Scrum Teams — you do not grow one team beyond ~10. The guide's rules for scaling:

  • One product = one Product Backlog = one Product Owner = one Product Goal. Several teams pull from the same backlog.
  • Teams working together on one product must mutually define and comply with the same Definition of Done, so their combined Increment is valuable and usable.
  • Cross-team dependencies are minimized by organizing around features/value (feature teams) rather than components.
⭐ From your class quiz "Many Scrum Teams on a single product — DoD?" → All teams must have a Definition of Done that makes their combined Increment valuable and useful (a shared DoD; individual teams may add stricter criteria on top).

From the lecture's "Bigger product = more teams" slide: the teams may even run different Sprint cadences (Team 1 ships 3 sprints while Team 3 runs one long one) — what stays shared is the one Product Backlog/Goal they all sync against.

Scrum antipatterns — "Scrum in name only"

📌 The lecture's definition (memorize this) 📜 verbatim "In general, anti-patterns are common practices that are seen initially convenient and appropriate, but that are typically harmful in the long run and should, therefore, be avoided." An anti-pattern can relate to a single or several Scrum elements. The underlying assumption: following the Scrum Guide enables the team to realize the agile benefits — NOT following it may reduce or reverse the effect.
⭐ Your professor's 5-question analysis framework (used in class — likely exam structure) For any anti-pattern, be able to answer:
  1. In which way does it deviate from the Scrum Guide?
  2. Which event, role, or artifact is affected?
  3. What are the reasons for the deviation?
  4. What impact (positive/negative) does it have on overall agility?
  5. Any recommendation on how to handle the situation?
🧠 Memory hook The 5 questions = D-E-R-I-R: "Did Everyone Read It Right?" (Deviation from the Guide · Element affected · Reasons · Impact · Recommendation.)

⭐ The 8 case-study antipatterns from your course handout

🧠 Memory hook — the 8 cases as one story "A fake Owner (1) with no power (2) counted hours (3) while customers sneaked in (4); nobody held retros (5), Sprints stretched (6) and grew too long (7), and testing came next Sprint (8)." — Notice the structure: 2 broken POs + 1 broken metric + 1 broken boundary + 1 missing event + 2 broken timeboxes + 1 broken quality.
1. Customer Product Owner
The customer installs their own (too busy) representative as PO → unclear requirements, organizational barriers, dictating HOW the team should implement, pushing new items mid-Sprint → reduced team commitment.
The PO is one empowered person close to the team who owns and orders the backlog; the PO communicates WHAT/WHY — never HOW (that's the Developers'); mid-Sprint scope is negotiated, never imposed. Affected: PO role, Sprint Backlog, Sprint.
2. Product Owner without authority
A team member is "promoted" to PO but can't decide what to build or discard → no prioritization (everything must be built), managers above override decisions, no direct customer contact → no idea what's valuable.
The organization must respect the PO's decisions; the PO orders the backlog by value and talks to customers/stakeholders directly. Affected: PO role, Product Backlog (ordering), Product Goal.
3. Hours in progress monitoring
Management/finance requires all stories estimated and reported in man-hours → heavy estimation effort, estimates wrong anyway (especially research-type stories), metrics serve accounting, not the team.
Estimation (sizing) belongs to the Developers in the format that helps THEM plan (e.g. relative sizing); progress toward the Sprint Goal is inspected at the Daily Scrum — artifacts exist for transparency, not external reporting. Affected: Sprint Backlog, Developers, Daily Scrum.
4. Customer caused disruption
Customers negotiate new features directly with individual Developers mid-Sprint ("good customer relations") → PO bypassed, undocumented work, Sprint Backlog no longer reflects reality (transparency destroyed).
All work flows through the Product Backlog owned by the PO — the single source of work; anyone wanting changes must convince the PO. Affected: PO role, Sprint Backlog, transparency pillar.
5. Business as usual (No Sprint Retrospective)
No SM (or SM acting as a traditional PM) → retros not held, or 15-minute token retros with no actionable items → no improvement, team questions Scrum's value and considers abandoning it.
The Retro concludes every Sprint (≤3h timebox); it must produce improvement actions — the most impactful addressed ASAP, possibly in the next Sprint Backlog. Affected: Retrospective event, SM role, adaptation pillar.
6. Varying Sprint length
Anyone can push items mid-Sprint, so Sprints are extended instead of ending on date → no progress visibility, problems surface late, no commitment to the Sprint Goal ("we can always extend"), no shipping date, ready features rot in version control, planning/review must be re-scheduled each time.
Sprints are fixed-length timeboxes and are NEVER extended; consistent cadence creates rhythm, predictability and discipline. Affected: the Sprint itself, Sprint Goal, inspection pillar.
7. Too long Sprint
Waterfall-legacy company stretches Sprints (customers dislike short cycles) → early weeks wasted, oversized tasks still unfinished, customers wait too long while priorities shift, more interruptions, less predictability.
Sprints are one month or LESS — shorter Sprints generate more learning cycles and limit risk/cost to a smaller time frame; large items are split during refinement. Affected: Sprint, Increment cadence.
8. Testing in the next Sprint
Separate test team tests in Sprint N+1 → new code built on untested code, Increment never "potentially shippable" at Sprint end, bugs found weeks later (high cognitive reload), handoffs create idle wait states and extra communication.
Quality is built IN: testing is part of the Definition of Done within the same Sprint by a cross-functional team — work that isn't Done returns to the Product Backlog. Affected: Increment + DoD, Developers, transparency.

More classic role antipatterns

The Scrum Master as taskmaster / team secretary
SM assigns tickets, runs the board, books rooms, chases status — the team never learns self-management.
The SM is a servant leader accountable for Scrum being understood and for team effectiveness; Developers self-select all work.
The absent / proxy / committee Product Owner
PO has no time, no authority over the backlog, or is several people — backlog rots, value decisions stall.
The PO is one empowered person, accountable for value and backlog order; the organization must respect their decisions.
The PM in disguise / "Scrummerfall" hierarchy
A project manager assigns work and reports progress upward; the team is a feature factory.
There is no PM in Scrum; the team is self-managing and cross-functional with three accountabilities only.
QA as a separate gate team
Testing happens "after dev" in a separate column/team — a mini-waterfall inside the Sprint; quality becomes someone else's job.
Quality is instilled by all Developers adhering to the Definition of Done; testers are Developers with a testing specialty.

Event antipatterns

Daily Scrum as status report
Developers report to the SM/PO/manager, ritually answering "the three questions"; no plan is adapted.
The Daily Scrum belongs to the Developers: inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal, adapt the Sprint Backlog, plan the day. Any format works.
Sprint Review as a one-way demo (or skipped because "nothing is finished")
Stakeholders passively watch slides; no feedback reaches the Product Backlog.
The Review is a working session with stakeholders to inspect the Increment and adapt the backlog.
Skipping the Retrospective ("no time", "nothing to improve")
The team loses its formal improvement loop — process debt accumulates.
The Retro concludes every Sprint; the most impactful improvements are addressed as soon as possible (may even enter the next Sprint Backlog).
Planning without a Sprint Goal / management dictates the Sprint scope
The Sprint becomes a load of unrelated tickets; nothing gives focus, nothing can flex.
Sprint Planning answers Why → What → How; Developers select the items they forecast they can do.
The "hardening" / testing / release Sprint
Increments aren't really Done each Sprint; work piles up undone and risk explodes at the end.
Every Sprint produces a usable, Done Increment — if it doesn't meet the DoD, it goes back to the Product Backlog.

Artifact & metric antipatterns

Sprint scope locked ("no changes once we start")
Confuses "protect the Sprint Goal" with "freeze everything"; kills learning inside the Sprint.
Scope is clarified and renegotiated with the PO as more is learned — only the Sprint Goal is fixed.
Velocity as a KPI / comparing teams by velocity
Velocity is a private planning aid; as a target it gets gamed (story-point inflation) — Goodhart's law.
Measure outcomes (value, working increments); velocity only helps a single team forecast.
Undone work counted as "done" (DoD theatre)
"Done except testing/docs/review" destroys transparency — stakeholders inspect a lie.
Work below the DoD is not part of the Increment, full stop.
Hero culture / siloed ownership ("only Maria touches the database")
Bus-factor 1, queues form behind one person, the team can't swarm on the Sprint Goal.
Cross-functional team; Sprint Backlog items are owned by the Developers collectively — never by one person.
How to attack ANY antipattern scenario at the exam — two quick tests, then the 5-question framework.
Spot-the-antipattern heuristic Ask: does the scenario reduce transparency, prevent inspection, or block adaptation? Does someone outside the accountability make the call (manager assigns work, SM owns backlog, PO skips the team)? That's your answer.

DevOps — extending agility to operations

📌 The lecture's definition (memorize this) 📜 verbatim "DevOps is a set of practices that automates the processes between development and IT teams, in order that they can build, test, and release software faster and more reliably. The concept of DevOps is founded on building a culture of collaboration between teams that historically functioned in relative siloes."

Where Scrum optimizes how a team plans and builds, DevOps optimizes how software flows to production and runs. They complement each other: Scrum gives the cadence, DevOps gives the pipeline. Slogan to remember (it's on the slides): "You build it, you run it."

⭐ Why we need DevOps (from the slides)

  • The wall between Dev and Ops: in plain Agile, the developer says "the code works on my system!" while Operations says "the same code doesn't work on mine!" — DevOps makes them work together so "code works on my system too."
  • Technical debt (slide definition) 📜 verbatim: "whenever software is developed in a partial way that later produces errors and artifacts that need to be addressed… the result of prioritizing speedy delivery over perfect coding." DevOps automation (testing, CI) keeps debt visible and small.
⭐ Class discussion: "Is DevOps an anti-pattern?" Be ready to argue both sides. Benefits: faster, more reliable releases; shared ownership; short feedback cycles; less integration risk. Challenges: on-call burden on developers (the Comic Agilé slide: "I hate DevOps!" at 3 a.m.), required skill breadth, cultural resistance, tooling investment, and blurred role boundaries. Conclusion: DevOps is not an anti-pattern per se — it's an enabler of agility — but applied without culture change ("a DevOps team silo") it can become one.

The DevOps infinity loop (know the 8 stages)

PLAN → CODE → BUILD → TEST → RELEASE → DEPLOY → OPERATE → MONITOR ─┐ ▲ (Dev side) (Ops side) │ └────────────────────── feedback flows back into planning ◄────────────────┘
The DevOps infinity loop — Dev side on the left, Ops side on the right, crossing at Test→Release. Monitoring closes the loop: production data feeds the next plan (and the Product Backlog).

What is a team — and why dynamics matter (Lasse Lui Frandsen's lecture) 📜 definition verbatim

A (project) team: a group of people that work together to achieve a common goal — individual & shared tasks, shared responsibility for the outcome, and they view themselves as a single unit.

Why we use teams

  • Better problem-solving

⭐ Tuckman's stages of group development (1965; +Adjourning 1977) 📜 near-verbatim

A model of how groups form and develop. Groups do not skip stages, but can move back and forth. Knowing the stages helps the team reach later stages and avoid regressing. Overall trajectory: individual autonomy → team autonomy · low cohesion → high cohesion · getting used to the team → working effectively.

Tuckman's stages as a performance curve. Groups can't skip stages but CAN slide back (e.g. a new member can push a performing team back to storming) — the GDQ tells you where you are.
🧠 Memory hook — the 5 S's of the Scrum Master One S per Tuckman stage: Set up (Forming: goals, fit, small team) → Settle (Storming: trust, conflict, structure) → Support (Norming: cohesion, ownership) → Step back (Performing: remove barriers only) → Send off (Adjourning: closure). And the stages themselves rhyme: form, storm, norm, perform — then adjourn.

Group Development Questionnaire (GDQ) (Wheelan & Hochberger 1996; short version Gren et al. 2020): a questionnaire to identify which stage a group is in — so support/intervention can match the stage. Use: identify stage → anticipate the stage's common challenges → support the move to the next stage → watch for regression risks.

Historical context (RP: Bonebright 2010, "40 years of storming"): Tuckman's 1965 model (forming–storming–norming–performing) became "the most predominantly referred to" group development model; Adjourning was added in the 1977 revision with Jensen.

  • Trailblazers: use GenAI for more complex tasks (not just drafting), master prompting skills, and create adoption momentum.
  • Organizational enablement is key: clear vision, strategic goals and leadership around GenAI → empowered professionals → utilization. Challenges: access, data reliability, cost, ethics, accountability.

⭐ Niederman (2021) "PM: openings for disruption from AI and advanced analytics"

  • Critical essay: PM "pull" meets AI/analytics "push" → incremental AND disruptive evolution of PM.
  • AI reaches PMs mostly through packaged PM software, in four escalating ways: (1) support current activities, (2) streamline/eliminate (automate) activities, (3) extend capabilities with more data/computation, (4) reconceive how projects are done — and whether they're needed at all.
  • Practical implication: PMs (unless building AI products) don't need deep AI skills — but must scan for new software features/affordances that unlock productivity.

⭐ Wolpers (Scrum.org, 2025) "The Dangerous Middle: agile roles AI will erode first"

  • Builds on Peter Yang: AI splits roles into generalists who prototype end-to-end with AI and top-5% specialists — everyone in the "dangerous middle" gets squeezed.
  • At risk: practitioners whose core value is running ceremonies and maintaining backlogs — "Scrum Master as Secretary", "PO as Backlog Administrator", "Agile Coach as Framework Preacher". If a playbook can capture it, software can replicate it.
  • The defensible edge: integrative mastery — data analysis + organizational psychology + systems thinking + political skill; use AI to scale diagnosis but reserve judgment for yourself; move value "from process to change leadership". Agency is the moat.
Yang's polarization applied to agile roles (Wolpers 2025): the curve is your market value. Escape the dip by moving left (AI-augmented generalist) or right (diagnostic change leader) — agency is the moat.

Hughes et al. (2025) — PM2030 opinion paper

Multi-expert perspectives on AI's impact on PM toward 2030. The central question to discuss at the exam: will AI take the lead in key decisions, or will human attributes — creativity, ethical judgment, emotional intelligence — remain essential?

⭐ One-line synthesis for the oral exam AI automates the mechanics of PM (reporting, estimating, facilitation templates) → value shifts to the human core: judgment, trust-building, change leadership. Same conclusion from PMI (upskill now), Niederman (watch the tooling), and Wolpers (escape the dangerous middle).

RP1 papers

Bonebright (2010) — 40 Years of Storming: a historical review of Tuckman's model
Literature review tracing Tuckman's 1965 model (forming–storming–norming–performing; adjourning added with Jensen in 1977) from HRD practice into academia. It became "the most predominantly referred to and most widely recognized" group-development model — valued for simplicity, criticized for limited empirical validation and for assuming linear progression.
Links to: Team Dynamics lecture (the model itself, stages & SM roles).
Langfred (2000) — The paradox of self-management: individual and group autonomy
Field study of work groups in two organizations. Finding: individual autonomy and group autonomy have OPPOSITE effects on group cohesiveness — high individual autonomy lowers cohesion (members work apart), high group autonomy raises it. Effects on effectiveness are mediated by cohesiveness (interacting with the group's performance norms). Design lesson: you must balance BOTH levels of autonomy when designing self-managing teams.
Links to: self-managing agile teams; explains WHY a "team of lone wolves" isn't agile.
Marcelino-Sádaba et al. (2014) — Project risk management methodology for small firms
SMEs run survival-critical projects without PM professionals. Based on research with 72 Spanish companies, proposes a risk-driven methodology with simple tools, templates, and risk checklists (with recommended actions and indicators), explicitly covering what SMEs neglect: alignment with strategy and results management. Validated in 5 real projects (innovation, management systems, ICT).
Links to: lifecycle & planning lecture (risk management made proportionate to small scale).
Havermans et al. (2019) — Rolling Into the Profession: becoming a project manager
Qualitative interviews (31 PMs + 21 supervisors, NL & UK). PMs are drawn in by enterprising interests and mostly "roll into" the profession without knowing career paths; they experience both positive aspects (variety, responsibility) and negatives. Implication: organizations should make PM career paths deliberate rather than accidental.
Links to: Lecture 1 (the PM role) and your own career reflection — a favorite oral-exam tangent.
Cooper (2016) — Agile–Stage-Gate Hybrids
Invited essay: firms integrate Agile elements INTO their gating processes — first in IT, now for physical products. Benefits: faster releases, better response to changing requirements, improved communication & morale. Agile needs modifications for hardware (what a "done sprint" means). Two large-company best-practice examples.
Links to: Frameworks tab (Stage-Gate + hybrid section).

RP2 papers

Cooper & Sommer (2016) — Agile-Stage-Gate: new idea-to-launch method for manufactured products
Manufacturers (food to heavy equipment, incl. LEGO) integrating Agile with gating get faster, more responsive NPD — "the most significant change to gating systems in thirty years". Implementation = the "Power of Nine": 3 artefacts (sprints, scrums…), 3 tools (sprint backlog, burndown chart…), 3 roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master…). Adjustments required: redefining a "done sprint", presenting product versions to customers as models/prototypes.
Links to: Frameworks tab; pairs with Cooper (2016) above.
Shrivastava & Rathod (2015) — Categorization of risk factors for distributed agile projects
Distributed + agile development promises better/faster/cheaper but poses risks. From 13 practitioner interviews + 28 project documents: 45 risk factors grouped in 5 core categories (around the software lifecycle, project management, group awareness, external stakeholder collaboration, and technology setup), mapped to Leavitt's model of organizational change, plus the risk-management methods practitioners actually use.
Links to: risk management (Lecture 2) + distributed/virtual teams (Team Dynamics).
McHugh, Conboy & Lang (2012) — Agile Practices: the impact on trust in software project teams
Three case studies of agile teams. Iteration planning, daily stand-ups and retrospectives can ENHANCE trust among team members (visibility of commitments, regular communication) — while also generating new challenges (e.g. exposure/peer pressure). Uses Mayer's definition of trust: willingness to be vulnerable based on expectations of the other party.
Links to: Team Dynamics (trust) + Scrum events — the events are trust-building machinery.

RP3 papers

Azanha et al. (2017) — Agile PM with Scrum: Brazilian pharmaceutical IT case
Exploratory case study of a software project using Scrum. Benefits found: increased motivation and staff satisfaction, better control of requirements, higher quality — and dramatic speed: features usable from the first month, development in 4 months ≈ 30% of the estimated traditional duration (≈75% time reduction). Limitation: single case, not generalizable.
Links to: Scrum foundations — empirical evidence for "value sooner, less risk".
Kristensen & Paasivaara (2021) — What added value does a Scrum Master bring? (Nordea case)
Case study at Nordea (the bank from the SAFe lecture example). The SM role carries lots of "invisible" workload — removing impediments, shielding the team — letting developers focus and be productive; teams without an SM risk failure, yet organizations under-value the role. The role matures with the team: from teaching the method early on, to coaching the wider organization later, with developers gradually taking over parts.
Links to: SM accountability (Scrum tab), Spiegler's 9 roles / leadership gap (Team Dynamics), SAFe at Nordea (Scaling).
Moe, Dingsøyr & Dybå (2010) — A teamwork model for understanding an agile team
9 months of fieldwork in a company introducing Scrum, analyzed with Dickinson & McIntyre's teamwork model. Barriers to effectiveness: problems with team orientation, team leadership and coordination, plus highly specialized skills and division of work. Beyond the model's components, trust and shared mental models proved fundamental. Conclusion: moving from individual work to self-managing teams requires reorientation by developers AND management — it takes time and resources.
Links to: Team Dynamics tab (the quotes on the slides come from this paper).

RP4 + extra

Bremer, Rylander Eklund & Elmquist (2025) — Scaling or growing agile? A manifesto for agile organization development
Point-of-view paper using a Swedish software company's journey. Large-scale agile frameworks "scale" agile by adding structure — clashing with the Agile Manifesto's assumptions about uncertainty and trusting people. Alternative: GROW agile rather than scale it — a proposed Manifesto for Agile Organization Development with 4 values + 5 principles, bringing the original manifesto's mindset (trust people to solve problems together locally) to the organizational level. Not a blueprint, but a basis for local practice.
Links to: Scaling tab — the academic counterweight to SAFe's 53% adoption ("Doing vs Being agile").
EXTRA · Spiegler, Heinecke & Wagner (2020) — Leadership gap in agile teams: how teams and Scrum Masters mature
Grounded theory, 53 interviews, 29 teams at Bosch. SMs initially play nine leadership roles (Method Champion, Disciplinizer on Equal Terms, Coach, Change Agent, Helicopter, Moderator, Knowledge Enabler, Networker, Protector) and transfer roles to the team as it matures, deliberately creating a leadership gap that members grow into. Enablers: trust + freedom to take on leadership.
Links to: Team Dynamics tab (the 9-roles slide is from this research line) + Tuckman stages (SM behavior per stage).

🧠 The 60-second story of the whole course

One narrative chain that walks every tab in exam order. Read it out loud three times tonight and once tomorrow morning — then each keyword unlocks its whole topic. (Every hook below also appears in purple boxes inside its own tab, right next to the content.)

A sponsor signs a charter (the birth certificate) for a T.U. endeavor — Temporary & Unique. Scrum runs on the TIA engine (Transparency → Inspection → Adaptation) with 3-3-5 (3 accountabilities, 3 artifacts, 5 events) and the timebox rhythm "ate at quarter-past for tea" (8h/15′/4h/3h). "Can Five Owls Really Code?" = the five values. Many teams? The Rule of Ones and the ×5 fractal where the EAT eats impediments. Done badly, you get the antipattern story ("a fake Owner with no power counted hours…") — analyzed with "Did Everyone Read It Right?". Done well, DevOps ships it: "People Can Build Things; Robots Deploy, Operate, Monitor."

Underneath it all, a team climbs form-storm-norm-perform-adjourn while the SM does the 5 S's (Set up, Settle, Support, Step back, Send off), held together by the Glue, the Ground, the Wire and the Map. And above it all hovers AI — "Doctors Always Diagnose Carefully" — slowly eating the dangerous middle. The closing question of the course: silicon or soul?
